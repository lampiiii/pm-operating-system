# 🎯 Today's Persona Drip

**Scenario:** Building the SMB scale plan — the memo that decides whether a segment survives

**Time:** Tuesday, 2:00 PM

**Context:** A senior CSM has been asked by the VP of Customer Success to write a one-page memo on whether the SMB segment (sub-$20K ARR) should keep dedicated CSM coverage, move to a pooled model, or go fully self-serve. The decision affects 14 CSMs and 900 accounts. She has until Friday.

---

**The Play-by-Play:**

- Blocks her calendar 2–4 PM. Closes Slack. Opens a blank doc titled "SMB Coverage Model — Recommendation."
- Pulls the numbers first. 900 SMB accounts, $11.2M ARR. Current blended CSM cost: $1.8M annually. That's 16% of SMB revenue going to CSM coverage — triple the commercial segment ratio.
- Looks at the outcomes. SMB gross retention: 78%. Net retention: 84%. Commercial segment: 91% and 108%. The CSM investment isn't moving retention enough to justify itself.
- But then pulls the activation data. Accounts that get a CSM-led onboarding hit "activated" status (3+ features used, 5+ active users) at 64%. Self-serve onboarded accounts: 29%. The CSM drives activation, not retention.
- Sketches three models on paper:
  - **Model A — Keep dedicated**: status quo. Easy but expensive and unaccountable.
  - **Model B — Pooled**: 4 CSMs rotate on a shared queue, handle escalations and renewals only. Saves $1M. Risk: activation drops.
  - **Model C — Self-serve + pooled**: kill dedicated onboarding, invest savings into in-product activation. Highest upside, highest execution risk.
- Writes the memo with Model C as the recommendation — but structures it so the VP sees the tradeoffs of all three, not just the winner.
- Adds a "what I'd need to believe" section: for Model C to work, in-product onboarding activation rate needs to hit 50%+ within 6 months. Includes two leading indicators to watch monthly.
- Stops. Rereads. Realizes she's burying the headline in paragraph 3. Rewrites the top: "I recommend Model C — self-serve + pooled — because CSM spend in SMB isn't buying retention, it's buying activation, and activation can be re-engineered into the product."
- Adds the risks section honestly. The biggest risk isn't activation — it's CSM morale. 14 CSMs will read this memo and hear "your job is being restructured." She flags it explicitly.
- Sends to her VP at 3:52 PM with a note: "Happy to walk through the reasoning live. The morale question is the one I'm least sure about."

---

**The Invisible Friction:**

- The platform doesn't distinguish "CSM drove this outcome" from "CSM was assigned when this happened." Attribution is assumed, not measured.
- Activation metrics live in product analytics; retention metrics live in the CSM tool. The person building the memo has to stitch them together manually.
- There's no easy way to model the counterfactual: "what would SMB retention look like without CSM coverage?" Leaders decide on vibes because the data doesn't extend into what-if.

---

**PM Takeaway:**

Strategic CS work is about distinguishing correlation from causation with the data you actually have. If your platform lets CSMs prove "I drove this outcome," you're not just serving CSMs — you're giving CS leaders the ammunition to defend (or restructure) their own org. That's a much more valuable product.
